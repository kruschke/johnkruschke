  1. For normal population with mean = 6.0, SD = 2.5, and sample size n = 20, what is the probability that the sample mean will be greater than 7.0? Greater than 5.0?

  2. For IQ scores, the population is normal with mean = 100 and SD = 15. Consider two random samples, with n1 = 20 and n2 = 100. Which sample is more likely to have a sample mean greater than 110? Greater than 90?

  3. Suppose for a sample with n = 40, the sample mean is 6.0 and the sample SS is 3078. Compute the best estimate of the population mean and the 95% confidence interval. What assumptions have you made in computing this interval?

  4. Suppose for a sample with n = 20, the sample mean is 6.0 and the sample SS is 1539. Compute the best estimate of the population mean and the 95% confidence interval. What assumptions have you made in computing this interval?

  5. In a sample of n = 20, the 95% confidence interval for the mean was 875 to 955. What was the estimated standard error fo the mean? What was the estimated standard deviation of the population?

  6. Suppose we have samples from two conditions, with statistics as follows.
    Group A: n = 18, mean = 6.89, SS = 49.78
    Group B: n = 18, mean = 8.61, SS = 70.28
    Compute the 95% confidence interval for the difference in population means. What assumptions have you made?

  7. For samples from two conditions, each with n = 20, the difference of sample means was 8.70. In testing the null hypothesis, t = 2.30. What was the estimated standard error of the difference of means? What was the estimated standard deviation of the underlying groups in the population?

  8. Here are data of samples from two conditions:
    Group A: 1, 2, 2, 3.
    Group B: 3, 4, 4, 5.
    What is the 95% confidence interval for the difference of means? Conduct a t-test of the null hypothesis. What assumptions did you make for the test? What is Cohen's d? What is R2?

  9. Here are data of samples from two conditions:
    Group A: 1, 4, 4, 9.
    Group B: 9, 16, 16, 25.
    * What is the 95% confidence interval for the difference of means? Conduct a t-test of the null hypothesis. What assumptions did you make for the test? What is Cohen's d? What is R2?
    * To alleviate any doubt that the assumptions of the t-test are satisfied, transform all the scores with a square-root function. Are the assumptions of the t-test better satisfied with the transformed scores? Conduct a t-test; are the conclusions the same? (HINT: See the previous problem.)

  10. A sample of n = 21 is taken from each of three conditions. The samples show these statistics:
    Group A: mean = 13.7, SS = 158.
    Group B: mean = 12.6, SS = 161.
    Group C: mean = 8.2, SS = 193.
    * Calculate Tukey's HSD and find 95% confidence intervals for the three differences between means. Which means are significantly different?
    * Complete an ANOVA summary table. What is the critical F for a 5% type-I error rate? What is R2? What is the conclusion of the omnibus ANOVA and what assumptions have you made?
    * What is Fisher's LSD? Which means are significantly different?

  11. Samples are taken from three conditions, with raw scores as follows:
    Group A: 20, 22, 23, 23
    Group B: 21, 23, 25, 27
    Group C: 26, 28, 30, 30
    * Calculate Tukey's HSD and find 95% confidence intervals for the three differences between means. Which means are significantly different?
    * Complete an ANOVA summary table. What is the critical F for a 5% type-I error rate? What is R2? What is the conclusion of the omnibus ANOVA and what assumptions have you made?
    * What is Fisher's LSD? Which means are significantly different?

  12. For samples from two conditions, summary statistics were as follows.
    Group A: n = 12, mean = 9.74, SS = 2218.
    Group B: n = 12, mean = 6.63, SS = 2874.
    * Conduct a t-test of the null hypothesis.
    * Assuming that the true difference between means is the observed difference between sample means, what is the power of the test?
    * What is the smallest sample size per group that would yield a power of .8 ?

  13. Suppose someone claims that two different populations differ in IQ by an average of 5 points. In both populations, IQ scores are normally distributed with an SD of 15 points. Suppose you want to challenge the claim, as you believe that there is no significant difference in mean IQ. What sample size would you need to detect a mean difference of 5 points with power of .9 ?

  14. Suppose your universal theory of how things work predicts that condition B should exceed condition A by a mean score of 10.0. A competing theorist tests your theory by randomly sampling the two conditions, and she comes up with these data:
    Group A: 1, 11, 21, 31, 41.
    Group B: 11, 21, 31, 41, 51.
    On the basis of these data, the competing theorist concludes that your theory's prediction is disconfirmed. How might you rebutt the conclusion? More specifically, using these data to estimate the SD in the population, how big of a sample size (per group) would be needed to detect your claimed difference with a power of .9?
