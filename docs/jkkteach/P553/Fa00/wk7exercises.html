P553 Statistics in Psychology, Prof. Kruschke

Additional Exercises for Week 7

Suppose we flip two independent fair coins; say, a nickel and a dime.

Suppose we flip each coin once. What is the probability distribution of the difference between the outcomes; i.e., what is the probability distribution of #heads on nickel minus #heads on dime?

Suppose we flip each coin twice. What is the probability distribution of the difference between the outcomes; i.e., what is the probability distribution of #heads on nickel minus #heads on dime?

Suppose I flip each coin twice. On the nickel I get two heads. On the dime I get two tails. Is it reasonable to assume that the two coins have the same underlying probability of getting a head; that is, is the probability of this outcome greater than .05?

Hints: To answer these questions, it might help to make a table of the possible outcomes, like the tables of outcomes of two dice in earlier homeworks or labs. On the margins of the table, put the #heads of each coin, along with the probability of getting that #heads. In the body of the table, put the difference of the #heads, and the probability of getting that difference. Then combine cells with the same difference of the #heads.
