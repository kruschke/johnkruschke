When you roll a pair of dice, there is variability in the values that come up, from one roll to the next. What's the best model of this variability? We'll consider two candidate models. One "model" is simply your intuitive guess on each roll. Another model is the fixed value 7. For reasons to be divulged later in the course, we'll measure the amount of residual variance as the squared difference between the model prediction and the actual value of the dice.

Now we will generate a probability distribution for the possible outcomes of the dice. The goal is to understand that the most likely value is 7, and hence get a glimpse of why this value is the best model for the outcome.

There are 36 equally likely outcomes for a role of the dice, as indicated in table below. In the table, enter the sum of D1 and D2. (Actually it takes a few steps to show that each of the 36 combinations is equally likely. To wit: Each outcome of a single die has probability 1/6; i.e., p(D1=x) = 1/6 for x = 1,...,6, and p(D2=y) = 1/6 for y = 1,...,6. Because the outcomes of the two dice are independent, the probability of of any particular combination of outcomes is the product of the individual probabilities: p(D1=x and D2=y) = 1/6 * 1/6 = 1/36 for any x and y.)
