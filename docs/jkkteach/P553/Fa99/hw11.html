5A (3 pts): A researcher wants to know whether the sex of the first child from a couple is independent of the sex of the second born child from the same couple. The researcher compiles birth records from 100 families and finds the following results: 

                1st born
                 M    F
      2nd    M  30   20
      born   F  20   30
Can the researcher reject the null hypthesis of independence? Show your by-hand computations.

5B (3 pts): The researcher wants to know whether the proportional birth rates of boys and girls are equal. She consulted statistical records of large families and compiled results from 1000 families with 7 children. The researcher counted the number of families with 0 girls, the number with 1 girl, and so on, and found these results: 

    
       r    0   1   2   3   4   5   6   7
     freq  30  65 195 210 210 195  65  30
The researcher compared this obtained frequency distribution against the expected binomial distribution. Can the null hypothesis be rejected?

5C (3 pts): The researcher realized that the data in Part B can be used much more simply to test the same hypothesis. Just determine the total number of girls in the sample of 7000 children, and see if it differs significantly from 3500. Can the null hypothesis be rejected?

5D (2 pts): Don't the conclusions from Parts B and C conflict? How can the apparent difference be resolved? HINT: One possibility comes from Part A -- think about the assumptions in deriving the expected frequency distribution for part B. What other possible explanations are there?
