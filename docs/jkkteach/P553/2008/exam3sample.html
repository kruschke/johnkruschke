P553 Statistics in Psychology, Prof. Kruschke

Exam 3 (Final) Sample Questions

The exam is closed-book and closed-notes, but you are encouraged to use a calculator. There are 100 points possible, with points for each problem as marked. You have 120 minutes; please allocate your time carefully.

1. (35 pts total)
Suppose the annual salary of people who have completed at least one statistics course is compared with the annual salary of people who have never completed a statistics course. Here are the results from a random sample:

With stat's course: $25,790. $33,115. $42,521. $54,598.
Without stat's course: $36,598. $46,993. $60,340. $77,478.

1A (15 pts). Of the following data transformations, which is the best to use in this case? ln(x), x2, exp(x), x3, sin-1(x). Apply the best transformation and conduct a t-test of independent means. Assume a two-tailed test with Type I error rate of 5%. What are the assumptions of the test? Have they been satisfied?

1B (15 pts). Transform the raw scores to rank scores, and conduct a t-test on the rank scores. Assume a two-tailed test with Type I error rate of 5%. Does your conclusion agree with your conclusion from the previous Part?

1C (5 pts). Suppose we make no population assumptions at all, and treat the eight scores in the data as representative of the entire population, and conduct a randomization test. How many combinations of scores would we have to consider to generate the full sampling distribution? That is, what is the maximum number of discrete ``spikes'' in the sampling distribution of differences of means? (Determine the exact number explicitly, don't leave it as merely a factorial.)

2. (35 pts total) To solve these you'll need to consult a table of binomial probabilities. See the web page for a links to binomial tables.

2A (10 pts). Suppose we flip a coin seven times and it comes up heads six times. Can we reject the null hypothesis that the coin is fair? Assume a two-tailed test with Type I error rate at most 5%.

2B (15 pts). Suppose we have an alternative hypothesis that the coin is biased, with probability of heads p=.75. If we now assume a directional test, with Type I error rate at most 5% (or less), what is the power of the test? (Again, with N=7 and null hypothesis p=.5.)

2C (10 pts). Suppose we flip the coin 700 times and it comes up heads 400 times. Can we reject the null hypothesis that the coin is fair? Assume a two-tailed test with Type I error rate at most 5%.

Or maybe the exam will have it this way: Do all those using χ2. (Your results should match what you got when using the binomial, unless N is too small for the χ2 approximation.)

3. (30 pts total)

3A (15 pts). Suppose we toss a six-sided die 65 times, and observe the following frequency distribution: 

          outcome    1   2   3   4   5   6
          frequency 12  10  15   7  18   3
Can the null hypothesis that the die is fair be rejected? Assume a Type I error rate of 5%. (Be sure to show clearly the df you are using.) Has the test violated the conventional rules for a good approximation by the continuous χ2?

3B (15 pts). Suppose we have a 3-sector spinner and a coin. We want to know whether the flip of the coin and the spin of the spinner are independent. Here are results from 54 pairs of coin flips and spinner spins: 

                     Spinner
                    A   B   C
          Coin  H  13  16  10
                T   5   8   2
Can the null hypothesis of independence be rejected? Assume a Type I error rate of 5%. (Be sure to show clearly the df you are using. Conduct the complete test even if the conventional guidelines for approximating the continuous χ2 are violated. Are they violated?)
