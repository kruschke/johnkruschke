  1. Suppose you play "cut your losses" with a regular six-sided die. Losses are measured as the squared deviation between the guess and the actual outcome. Suppose Jack always guesses 5, and Jill always guesses 3. They role the die four times. Will Jack be the loser?

  2. Two neighborhoods are polled regarding opinions about a proposed property tax increase. In neighborhood A, 37% are in favor of the increase. In neighborhood B, 32% are in favor. Consider the following proposed explanations for this difference:
    I. Neighborhood B has more expensive houses in a higher tax bracket.
    II. Neighborhood B has more expensive properties, and neighborhood A was more recently canvassed with pro-tax leaflets.
    III. The differences occurred only by chance sampling; the neighborhoods really have the same proportion of residents in favor.
    Rank the explanations in terms of parsimony, from most parsimonious to least, and explain why.

  3. (A.) Consider this set of five scores: 1, 3, 4, 6, 7. What is the mean? What is the standard deviation (using n-1 in the denominator)? What is the standardized score of Y=1? Of Y=7?

    (B.) Suppose that the scores above were radii (radiuses) of circles. Convert the scores to the circles' areas (A = pi x r2). What is the mean area? The standard deviation? What is the standardized score of Y = pi x 12? Of Y = pi x 72?

    Is the mean of the circles' areas the same as the area of a circle with the mean radius?

    (C.) Convert the scores to the circles' circumference (C = pi x 2 x r). What is the mean circumference? The standard deviation? What is the standardized score of Y = pi x 2 x 1? Of Y = pi x 2 x 7?

    Is the mean of the circles' circumferencess the same as the circumference of a circle with the mean radius?

  9. On a given day, a small hospital has ten births, with 7 boys and 3 girls. That same day, a larger hospital has one hundred births, with 70 boys and 30 girls. Which outcome is more probable?

  10. What's the probability of getting a "head" for the flip of a fair coin? What's the probability of getting a "1" for the roll of a fair die? What's the probability of getting a "head" for the flip of a coin and getting a "1" for the roll of a die?

  11. Suppose you have 10 friends that need a ride, but you can only take 3 of them in your car. How many different sets of friends might you take?

    For the following problems, involving the binomial distribution, you should be able to solve them without referring to a table of binomial probabilities. That is, you are expected to memorize the binomial probability formula and know how to use it:

  12. Suppose we flip a fair coin 3 times. What is the probability of getting zero heads? 1 head? 2 heads? 3 heads?

  13. In a five-alternative multiple choice exam, the probability of answering correctly by chance is .2. If there are 10 questions, what is the probability that a student would get 8 or more correct purely by chance?

  14. Consider two fair six-sided dice. When we roll them, any "doubles" (1&1, 2&2, 3&3, etc.) is considered a success. What is the probability of getting exactly 1 success out of 6 rolls? Approximately what percentage of the time would you expect to get 67 or fewer successes out of 360 rolls? (For this last question, use the normal approximation to the binomial.
