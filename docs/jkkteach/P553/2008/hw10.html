P553 Statistics, Prof. Kruschke, Homework 10

P553 Statistics in Psychology
Homework 10: The Binomial Distribution
Due Tuesday 2 December 2008.

These exercises assume a dichotomous, nominally-scaled population, with the probability of success denoted "p" and the sample size denoted "N," unless otherwise stated.

See linked info about how to generate binomial and Poisson distributions in SPSS, and some hints.

1. (5 pts) Derive (by hand, showing your computations!) and graph three binomial distributions for p=.6: One for N=1, a second for N=2, and a third for N=5. Calculate the mean and SD of each of each distribution, and mark them on the graphs.

2. (5 pts total) Consider a multiple-choice test with 20 questions, each of which has 5 possible answers.
2A. (2 pts) Suppose a student gets 7 correct. Can the hypothesis that the student was randomly guessing be rejected? Use a two-tailed test.
2B. (3 pts) Suppose a student gets just 1 correct. Can the hypothesis of random guessing be rejected? Use a two-tailed test.

3. (5 pts total) Consider a null hypothesis of p=.5 and an alternative hypothesis of p=.7.
3A. (2 pts) For N=6, what is the power of the test?
3B. (3 pts) If we want a power of at least .8, what is the minimal N?

4. (5 pts total)
4A. (2 pts) A random sample of 100 students has 54 females and 46 males. Can the null hypothesis, that the student population has 50% females, be rejected?
4B. (3 pts) In a random sample of 100 students, none knew what the Poisson distribution is. Can the hypothesis, that 1.5% of the student population knows the definition of the Poisson, be rejected? What if 5 students in the sample of 100 knew the definition -- could the hypothesis be rejected?

5. (5 pts) Suppose we get the following sequence of heads (H) or tails (T) from flipping a coin:
H T H H T H H T H H H T T H H T H H H T T H H H H H T

  • Enter the results, as a column of letters, in SPSS. Use Transform -> Automatic Recode to recode the results into a numerical variable. Then use Analyze -> Reports -> Case Summaries to print the spreadsheet.
  • Use SPSS to test whether the coin is fair. Use Analyze -> Nonparametric Tests -> Binomial. (The built-in Help topics can be useful.) Test also the hypothesis that the probability of heads for this coin is 0.6 (instead of 0.5).
