Cut Your Losses, Episode 1.

When you roll a pair of dice, there is variability in the values that come up, from one roll to the next. What's the best description of a typical value for the dice? A description is also called a model. We'll consider two candidate models of the dice. One model is simply your intuitive guess on each roll. Another model is the fixed value 7. For purposes of this exercise, we'll measure the discrepancy between the model and the actual value as the squared difference between the model prediction and the actual value of the dice. This discrepancy is the loss you are trying to cut; i.e., the best model should cut your losses to the minimal possible amount.

Now we will generate a probability distribution for the possible outcomes of the dice. The goal is to understand that the most likely value is 7, and hence to get a glimpse of why this value is the best model of the outcome. There are 36 equally likely outcomes for a role of the dice, as indicated in the table at the right. In the table, enter the sum of D1 and D2.

(Actually it takes a few steps to show that each of the 36 combinations is equally likely. To wit: Each outcome of a single die has probability 1/6; i.e., p(D1=x) = 1/6 for x = 1,...,6, and p(D2=y) = 1/6 for y = 1,...,6. Because the outcomes of the two dice are independent, the probability of of any particular combination of outcomes is the product of the individual probabilities: p(D1=x and D2=y) = 1/6 * 1/6 = 1/36 for any x and y.)
