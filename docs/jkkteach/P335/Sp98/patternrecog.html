Brief Overview of Lectures on Pattern Recognition

Real-Life Phenomena: Constancies and Context Effects. Any complete theory of human pattern recognition must account for both constancies and context effects. A "constancy" is a constant percept despite changes in the stimulus. For example, we recognize both A and A as the same (constant) letter, despite changes in size. A "context effect" is a change in the percept depending on the context. For example, the symbol /-\ can be seen as an A or an H depending on context:
T/-\E C/-\T

Applications: Computers can recognize some patterns, but most are very constrained in their abilities. What makes human pattern recognition so much richer?

Experiments: Templates, Feature Lists, and Structural Relations. The lectures spent a lot of time describing how experiments fit into the knowledge organization diagram at left. We discussed experimental evidence for the existence of templates, feature lists, and structural relations in human pattern recognition.

One of the main goals of these early lectures was to give you several thorough examples of the knowledge organization tree, so you can apply it yourself when learning about other experiments.

Context Effects: "Bottom-up" context effects occur despite any knowledge of the observer. For example, a diamond inside a tilted rectangle looks like a tilted square, regardless of any knowledge about geometric objects. "Top-down" context effects depend on the observer's knowledge. An example is the word superiority effect in letter recognition.

Context effects should not be construed as perceptual errors or infelicities; instead, the human visual system often achieves its constancies by taking advantage of the rich informational constraints supplied from context. For example, in lecture I showed the cover of the Beatles' album, Sgt. Pepper's, which has a crowd of faces on it. A computer program failed to detect many faces and falsely recognized some elbows as faces. People don't make those mistakes because we use the context to influence recognition.
