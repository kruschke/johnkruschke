Exam 2 Review Topics

Reading for this exam includes Reed pp.79-191.

Essay Question Topics:

  • The experiment by DeGroot on STM of chess positions by experts or novices (lecture and Reed pp.88-90).
  • Release from proactive interference, and the implication for STM/LTM distinction (lecture demo and Reed pp.84-86).
  • The experiment by Craik and Tulving on depth of processing, and implications for how to study for exams (lecture demo and Reed pp.154-157).
  • The experiment by E. Loftus regarding the effects of post-event information on eye-witness recognition, and factors that influence how easily post-event info is bonded with original memory (lecture demo).

Multiple Choice Question Topics:

  • The Peterson and Peterson (a.k.a. Brown/Peterson) paradigm for measuring the duration of STM (lecture demo and Reed pp.81-82).
  • The Waugh and Norman experiment that tested whether decay or interference was the dominant cause of forgetting in STM (lecture and Reed pp.82-84).
  • The experiment on STM scanning by Sternberg and serial-exhaustive vs. serial self-terminating models (homework, lecture and Reed pp.104-107).
  • Understand the main point of the experiments by Brown et al. (1977) regarding eyewitness identification and exposure to mug shots (Reed pp.134-136).
  • Semantic vs. auditory codes in patient P.V. (Reed pp.148-149).
  • The levels-of-processing approach and its criticisms (Reed pp.157-164).
  • Distinctiveness in memory (Reed pp.161-164).
  • Encoding specificity and state dependency (lecture and Reed pp.165-169).
  • The cognitive interview (lecture [in passing] and Reed pp.128-129, box 5.1 on pp.130-131).
  • Transfer appropriate processing (lecture and Reed pp.169-170).
  • Indirect vs. direct tests of memory (lecture and Reed pp.136-140)
  • Characteristics of amnesia, and implications for theoretical divisions of memory (lecture).
  • Fabricated vs. veridical suppressed memories (lecture).
  • The mnemonist, "S." (lecture).
  • Interactive imagery in advertising (lecture and Reed pp.178-180).
  • The method of loci (Reed pp.188-191).
  • Three themes of memory, and examples of each (lecture).
