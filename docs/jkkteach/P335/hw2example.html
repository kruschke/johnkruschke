Example of Model Calculations for Homework 2

Figure 1 (below) illustrates the calculations for activation propagation and weight learning in the model of Homework 2. The three training trials are not exactly the same sequence as in your homework, so the numerical values are not necessarily the same as what you should obtain in your homework. This example is intended to explicitly demonstrate the type of calculations you should do.

Figure 1. Illustration of activation propagation and weight learning for three training trials and a test trial.

For Training Trial 1, notice that all the weights start at zero, and the predicted activations are therefore both zero.

For Training Trial 2, the weight from stiff neck to A is now .5 because the learning from Trial 1 increased the weight from 0 to .5. Notice that the learning on this trial causes weights from both stiff neck and from pallid skin to increase by .25 because both of these symptoms are present. They increase by .25 because lrate*(tA-aA)*as = .5*(1-.5)*1 = .25.

For Training Trial 3, the weights to B start at 0, because nothing has been learned about B yet. Both weights increase by .5 as a result of this training trial.

(On your homework, there are additional training trials like Trial 2 and Trial 3. On these additional trials, the predicted activations of the diseases exactly match the teacher values. Consequently, there is no more change in the weights. If the learning rate were smaller than .5, say .1, then it would take more learning trials until the predicted activations matched the teaching values.)

For the Testing Trial, pallid skin and double vision are activated. Notice that disease B is activated more strongly than disease A because of the stronger weight from double vision than from pallid skin. There is no change in weights on this trial because there is no teacher value provided.
