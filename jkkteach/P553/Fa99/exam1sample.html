P553 Statistics in Psychology, Prof. Kruschke

Sample Exam 1

The exam is closed-book and closed-notes, but you are encouraged to use a calculator. There are 100 points possible, with points for each problem as marked. Copies of the normal-distribution table will accompany the exam (please do not write on these tables, as they will be re-used for future exams). You have 75 minutes; please allocate your time carefully.

  1. (10 pts.): State the English translation of Ubi dubium ibi libertas. Briefly explain how it relates to statistics, with respect to both dubium and libertas.

  2. (25 pts. total):

    (a, 5 pts) In a distribution skewed to the left, which is larger, the median or the mean? Why?

    (b, 5 pts) Suppose a sample contains the following seven scores: 1, 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4. What are the mode, median and mean values?

    (c, 10 pts) State the definitions of mode, median and mean in terms of the measures of variability that each minimizes. For the sample in Part (b), above, compute the value of the minimal variability for each measure of central tendency.

    (d, 5 pts) Define what is meant by the "maximal likelihood estimate" of a parameter value. If we model a population as a normal distribution with mean mu, what is the maximal likelihood estimate of mu in terms of the sample scores?

  3. (25 pts. total):

    (a, 5 pts) For a given population distribution, what does the sampling distribution of the mean look like when the sample size is 1? (describe in words)

    (b, 5 pts) What does the sampling distribution of the mean look like when the sample size approaches infinity? (describe in words)

    (c, 5 pts) Briefly state the Central Limit Theorem for the sampling distribution of the sample mean.

    (d, 10 pts) Suppose we have a population with mean mu = 5.3 and standard deviation sigma = 2.0. If we sample from that population with sample size N = 4, what is the expected value of the sample mean? What is the standard deviation (standard error) of the sampling distribution of the mean?

  4. (40 pts. total):

    Suppose we have a null hypothesis that mu0 = 100.0 and sigma0 = 15.0, and an alternative (research) hypothesis that mu1 = 95.0 (with the same sigma). We assume that the populations are normally distributed.

    (a, 5 pts) What is the effect size for these hypotheses?

    (b, 10 pts) Using a Type I error rate of alpha = .05, and a sample size of N = 9, what is the power of the test? (Assume a directional, one-tailed test.)

    (c, 10 pts) If we wanted the power to be at least .84, what minimal sample size would we need?

    (d, 5 pts) We draw a random sample from the world, with N = 9, and obtain the following scores: 75, 78, 82, 87, 93, 97, 105, 108, 115. Can we reject the null hypothesis? (The null and alternative hypotheses were stated at the beginning of this set of exercises. Assume a directional test with alpha = .05.)

    (e, 10 pts) Suppose that for a sample of 9 scores, the mean is 93.3. Using the standard deviation assumed by the null hypothesis, compute the 95% confidence interval for the population mean. (Assume a non-directional, two-sided confidence interval.)
