The Central Limit Theorem.

The central limit theorem says that as the sample size gets large, the probablity distribution of the sample statistic approaches a normal distribution. The central limit theorem applies to sample means: As the sample size increases, the mean of the sample is distributed approximately normally. Moreover, the variation of the sample mean from the true mean tends to decrease. We will roll dice to observe the central limit theorem for means.

Sample size N=1. Roll a die 15 times and record the results in the first column below. This is 15 samples with a sample size of 1. What is the mean of each sample? Because there is only one score per sample, the mean of the sample is simply the value of the single score. On a separate piece of paper, make a histogram of these 15 means, using bins of 0.51-1.50, 1.51-2.50, 2.51-3.50, 3.51-4.50, 4.51-5.50, and 5.51-6.50. You thereby have a (very) rough indication of the sampling distribution of the mean for a sample size of 1.

Sample size N=3. Next, increase the sample size to 3. If we were gluttons for boredom, we could roll 3 dice another 15 times, but instead, just use the values you and your neighbors already generated. So, in the next three columns of the table, enter the means of the corresponding three outcomes, along with the means from two of your neighbors. On a separate piece of paper, make a histogram of these 15 means, using bins of 0.51-1.50, 1.51-2.50, 2.51-3.50, 3.51-4.50, 4.51-5.50, and 5.51-6.50. You thereby have a (very) rough indication of the sampling distribution of the mean for a sample size of 3.

Sample size N=5. Next, increase the sample size to 5. If we were gluttons for boredom, we could roll 5 dice another 15 times, but instead, just use the values you and your neighbors already generated. So, in the next five columns of the table, enter the means of the corresponding five outcomes, along with the means from four of your neighbors. On a separate piece of paper, make a histogram of these 15 means, using bins of 0.51-1.50, 1.51-2.50, 2.51-3.50, 3.51-4.50, 4.51-5.50, and 5.51-6.50. You thereby have a (very) rough indication of the sampling distribution of the mean for a sample size of 5.
