The Poisson Approximiation to the Binomial Distribution

When p is extreme, the binomial is skewed (non-normal), and we approximate it with the discrete Poisson distribution:

pr(x;m) = ( exp(-m) mx ) / x!

for integer-valued x and m greater than zero. For the approximation to the binomial, we set m = N p, and x corresponds to r.

The example discussed in class: Suppose we have an assembly line for ball bearings. We hypothesize a defect rate of 1 in 10,000; i.e., p=0.0001. We sample 25,000 ball bearings and find that 4 are defective. Can we reject the hypothesis?

Out of N=25,000, we expect on average to find N*p=2.5 defective ball bearings, according to the hypothesis. In fact we found r=4, i.e., more than what we expected. Is this so extreme that we would reject our hypothesis?

In principle, we could use the binomial distribution. We would sum up the probability of getting r=4 or more extreme and see whether that is less than 2.5% (for a two-tailed test with 5% Type I error). The total probability of getting 4 or more is then

but this is intractable because of the huge factorials.

So, instead we use the Poisson approximation to the binomial, setting m=N*p=2.5. First, a trick: The probability that r is 4 or greater is just 1 minus the probability that r is 3 or less. Thus, the probability that r is 4 or more is

1 - [ pr(r=0) + pr(r=1) + pr(r=2) + pr(r=3) ]

= 1 - [ exp(-2.5)*2.50/0! + exp(-2.5)*2.51/1! + exp(-2.5)*2.52/2! + exp(-2.5)*2.53/3! ]

= 0.2424...

Thus, the probability of getting an r of 4 or more is about 24%, which means that we do not reject the hypothesis.
