The Central Limit Theorem.

The central limit theorem says that as the sample size gets large, the probablity distribution of the sample statistic approaches a normal distribution.

Consider flipping a coin N times. We learned in class that as N gets large, the distribution of the number of successes, r, becomes normal, with mean Np and standard deviation sqrt(Npq). Instead of measuring the number of successes, r, measure the proportion of successes, r/N. Then the mean is Np/N = p, and the standard deviation is sqrt(Npq)/N = sqrt(pq/N). In particular, notice that the mean proportion, p, does not change when N changes, but the standard deviation, sqrt(pq/N), gets smaller as N increases. (This decrease in standard deviation is really just a case of the law of large numbers.)

The central limit theorem also applies to sample means: As the sample size increases, the mean of the sample is distributed approximately normally. Moreover, the variation of the sample mean from the true mean tends to decrease. We will roll dice to observe the central limit theorem for means.

Roll a die 15 times and record the results in the first column below. This is 15 samples with a sample size of 1. What is the mean of each sample? Because there is only one score per sample, the mean of the sample is simply the value of the single score. On a separate piece of paper, make a histogram of these 15 means, using bins of 0.51-1.50, 1.51-2.50, 2.51-3.50, 3.51-4.50, 4.51-5.50, and 5.51-6.50. You thereby have a (very) rough indication of the sampling distribution of the mean for a sample size of 1.

Next, increase the sample size to 3. If we were gluttons for boredom, we could roll 3 dice another 15 times, but instead, just use the values you and your neighbors already generated. So, in the next three columns of the table, enter the means of the corresponding three outcomes, along with the means from two of your neighbors. On a separate piece of paper, make a histogram of these 15 means, using bins of 0.51-1.50, 1.51-2.50, 2.51-3.50, 3.51-4.50, 4.51-5.50, and 5.51-6.50. You thereby have a (very) rough indication of the sampling distribution of the mean for a sample size of 3.
