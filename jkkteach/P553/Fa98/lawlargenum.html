The Law of Large Numbers.

The law of large numbers says that the observed proportion in a sample will tend to more accurately reflect the true proportion in the population when the the sample size is larger (assuming that the sample was drawn randomly from the population).

We will roll dice to observe the law of large numbers in action.

Consider a regular, six sided die, with faces numbered 1 to 6. Half of its faces have odd numbers, and half have even numbers. Thus, for a fair, unbiased die, the proportion of ways it can come up odd is 0.5. We can denote this formally by saying that for the population of rolls of a fair die, the probability of an odd value outcome is p = 0.5.

When we actually roll the die any finite number of times, however, the proportion of times that it comes up odd valued will usually not be exactly 0.5. Denote the number of rolls as N, and denote the proportion of rolls that are odd as P = #odd/N.

The law of large numbers states that the difference between the true probability p and the sample proportion P tends to get smaller as the sample size N increases. Formally, the law of large numbers says that for any given critical deviation delta between P and p, the probability that the sample proportion lies within this deviation goes to 1.0 as the sample size increases more and more. The goal of this exercise is to make this last statement sensible and concrete.

You will roll a die a certain number of times, N, and tally the number of times it comes up odd or even. From this tally, compute the proportion, P, of times it came up odd in your sample (P = #odd/[#odd+#even] = #odd/N). Then determine whether this sample proportion is within an arbitrary critical deviation, delta = .21., from the true probability.
