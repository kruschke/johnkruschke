Exam 3 Review Topics (updated 3:20 pm 11/12/99)

Reading for this exam includes Chapters 8, 9, 10 and 11.

  • Strategies, such as conservative focussing, in discovering relevant attributes for conjunctive rules (lecture, see also Reed pp.224-225).
  • Know the meaning of conjunctive, disjunctive, conditional and biconditional logical rules (p. 222).
  • Know what types of concept rules are easiest and hardest to learn (Reed pp.221-224).
  • How concept learning and scientific method are, or are not, analogous (lecture and web notes).
  • Shared features among instances of super-ordinate, basic, and sub-ordinate categories (lecture and Reed pp.227-229).
  • Basic level categories and average shape of instances (Reed pp.230-231).
  • Family resemblance and typicality ratings (lecture and Reed pp.231-234).
  • Goal-derived categories and ideals (p. 234).
  • Prototype effects observed by Posner and Keele (1968; pp.241-243, Fig. 8.7).
  • Brain damage and category loss (Reed pp. 235-239).
  • Models of categorization (homework, lecture, web notes, and Reed pp.239-248).
  • Building semantic networks and subsequent test performance (pp. 260-261).
  • The hierarchical (or category size) effect, the reverse hierarchical effect, the typicality effect, the relatedness effect, and the fan effect (lecture, web notes, and Reed pp.262-278).
  • Semantic priming (p. 272).
  • Similarities and differences among the four models of semantic organization (lecture, web notes, and Reed pp.264-278).
  • Know how the ACT model accounts for the fan effect (lecture, web notes, and Reed pp. 273-275).
  • Schematization of memory (Reed pp. 278-280, lecture).
  • Accessing info in mental scripts: centrality vs. sequentiality in retrieval of events (Reed pp. 281-284, lecture).
  • ELIZA and the Turing test (lecture and web links [see schedule page]).
  • Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia (pp. 294-295).
  • Speech errors and types of exchanges (pp. 297-298).
  • Spoonerisms and other slips of the tongue, implications for the structure of speech, and Dell's model (lecture and pp. 297-298).
  • Chomsky's theory of transformational grammar (pp. 300-302, lecture) and MacKay's (1966) experiment on sentence ambiguity (pp. 302-304).
  • Swinney's research on contextual priming of ambiguous words (pp. 308-309).
  • Individual differences in resolving text ambiguities and Carpenter and Daneman's (1981) model for reading individual words (Reed pp.309-312).
  • Implications in advertising (pp. 317-318, lecture).
  • The role of schemas and scripts in language comprehension and memory (lecture and pp. 326-335).
  • Bransford and Johnson's experiments with abstract passages and context provided by pictures (pp. 326-328).
  • Anderson and Pichert's studies on perspective and recall of text (lecture, pp. 329-330).
  • Three determinants of comprehension difficulty (pp. 339-342).
  • Carpenter and Daneman's model of reading (pp. 309-311) compared with Kintsch's model of text comprehension (pp.342-349).
  • Perceptual span in reading; maximal reading speed (lecture).
  • Aphasic comprehension of the president's speech (lecture).
