Exam 3 Review Topics, Updated 7 November 1996

First, be sure to review the instructional goals of weeks 8 through 11. And of course, know how to use the knowledge organization diagram in the syllabus.

Essay Question Topics:

  • The maze running demonstration from class, and how the result can be explained by imagistic (analogue) representation and how it can be explained by propositional (symbolic) representation.
  • The experiment by Lutz & Lutz on imagery in advertising.
  • How concept learning and scientific method are (or are not) analogous.
  • How Rosch et al. found evidence that there is a basic level in natural categories. Specifically know both the attribute-listing experiment and the shape-identification experiment (see the textbook).
  • Know how the feature-comparison model accounts for the relatedness effect in sentence verification.
  • Know how the ACT model accounts for the fan effect.

Multiple Choice Question Topics:

  • Know how imagery can be used to improve athletic performance.
  • Know about the effects of interactive imagery in free recall and in associate-cued recall.
  • Know how imagery can explain the results of the Shepard and Metzler mental rotation experiment.
  • Know what "reality monitoring" is, and how good people are at it.
  • Know about the memorability of imagable (concrete) words. (Reed pp. 181-184.)
  • Know the Smith & Nielson face matching experiment, and how its results were explained.
  • Know about rule-learning strategies in concept learning.
  • Know what types of concept rules are easiest and hardest to learn.
  • Know about the extent of feature overlap in super-ordinate, basic, and sub-ordinate categories.
  • Know what models of categorization can account for prototypicality effects.
  • Know what family resemblance is (in the context of categorization).
  • Know the difference between path priming (Collins & Quillian) and semantic-associate priming (Meyers & Schvaneveldt).
  • Know what various effects the spreading activation model can or cannot account for.
  • Know the hierarchical (or category size) effect, the reverse hierarchical effect, the typicality effect, the relatedness effect, and the fan effect.
  • Know how the ACT model deals with effects of integration of sentences.
  • Know about the influence of causal relations on the fan effect. (Oops! In the 4th edition of Reed's book, this information has been moved from Chapter 9 to Chapter 11. Please read from the third paragraph of p.337 through the first paragraph of p.338, regarding Table 11.2.)
