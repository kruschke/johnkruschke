Relevance Papers

As discussed in the syllabus, one of the goals of this course is for you to discover the relevance of cognitive psychology to everyday life. The "relevance paper" assignments are designed to help you achieve this goal. (These papers are also referred to as "application" papers.)

The required structure of the papers.

Your paper should first describe the cognitive aspects of some life situation. The situation could be something you have personally experienced, but could also be taken from a movie, or from observations of animals, or even from observations of computers. The crucial qualities are that the situation should (1) be from life outside the research laboratory, and (2) involve some sort of putative cognition.

Next, your paper should describe two implications for the life situation derived from theories or experimental results in cognitive psychology. For each implication, you must accurately summarize the relevant theory or result, concisely describe its implication for the life situation, and describe a possible test of the implication.

When describing an experimental result, be sure to describe the stimuli, task, procedure, independent variables and dependent variables.

When describing a theory, be sure to describe the representation and process assumed by the theory, if possible.

In outline form, the paper is structured as follows:

  • Description of cognitive aspects of a life situation.
  • Implications from cognitive psychology:
    1. Implication #1.
      • Statement of theory or laboratory result.
      • Specific implication for life situation.
      • Possible test of the implication.
    2. Implication #2.
      • Statement of theory or laboratory result.
      • Specific implication for life situation.
      • Possible test of the implication.
Below is an example of a paper. This is only an abbreviated sketch of a paper, and it is incomplete in its details. The papers you submit should be written more carefully. Because I have provided this outline, you may not write a paper on this topic (imagery in athletic practice) for credit!

Sample Abbreviated Paper:
Mental Imagery in Learning to Ice Skate
  • Description of cognitive aspects of a life situation: Mental rehearsal of ice skating.

    In trying to improve performance in a new skill such as ice skating, I find that it helps to imagine skating when off the ice. My imagery emphasizes smooth, fluid execution of various maneuvers. This is not just fantasized glory, but concentrated mental rehearsal of specific realistic actions. This mental rehearsal seems to form a cognitive "rut" that can be more easily followed when I subsequently perform the actual, physical skating. (For more information about this phenomenon, see the newspaper article reprinted on pp. 202-203 of Reed, 4th Ed.)

  • Implications from cognitive psychology.

    1. Implication #1: Failure of reality monitoring.

      • Statement of theory or laboratory result.

        In an experiment conducted by Johnson, Raye, Wang and Taylor (1979), participants were shown either pictures of objects or names of these objects. When shown a name, the participant was instructed to imagine the object named. Two important independent variables were the number of times an object's picture appeared, and the number of times an object's name appeared. After exposure to the pictures and names, participants were given an unexpected test in which they had to estimate the number of times they saw an item's picture. The crucial dependent variable, therefore, was the judged number of times a picture was seen. The results were that estimates increased not only as a function of the actual number of times a picture was presented, but also as a function of the number of times the object was merely imagined. Thus, people confused imagining a picture with actually seeing a picture. (For more details, see pp. 210-214 of Reed, 4th Ed.)

      • Specific implication for life situation.

        This result suggests that if people vividly imagine performing an athletic skill such as skating, they will have difficulty distinguishing the number of times that they actually went skating from the number of times that they merely imagined skating. It also implies that if people imagined some particular action, such as a jump, then their estimates of the number of times they've actually jumped should be inflated by the number of times they've merely imagined jumping. In other words, people won't be able to fully distinguish reality from imagination. This implication extrapolates the laboratory situation, which was based on memory for external static objects, to a real life situation that involves internal action.

      • Possible test of the implication.

        This implication could be tested by having different participants in a skating class perform certain maneuvers (such as turns, stops, jumps, etc.) a specific number of times, and also having them imagine these maneuvers a certain number of times. After the class, students would be asked to estimate how many times they actually executed the various maneuvers. The implication would be confirmed if people's estimates were positively influenced by the number of times they imagined the maneuvers.

    2. Implication #2: Mental imagery might have a speed limit.

      • Statement of theory or laboratory result.

        Shepard and Metzler found that response times, to match two complex figures, increased linearly with the angle of rotation between the two figures. One explanation of their result is that mental rotation has a maximal speed; consequently, larger angles demand greater time to mentally traverse. (In your paper, you would explicitly specify the stimuli, task, independent and dependent variables, etc. For more details, see pp.197-199 of Reed, 4th Ed.)

      • Specific implication for life situation.

        Does this imply that there is a speed limit for athletic imagery as well? That is, does the speed limit apply only to complex geometric figures, or does the speed limit also apply to imagery of personal action? For example, if imagining a spin during a jump, will imagination be able to keep up with real life spinning? Will athletes sometimes imagine things too slowly? And if so, will this have detrimental effects instead of beneficial effects, contrary to what coaches believe?

      • Possible test of the implication.

        To test this implication, we first need to determine whether people can imagine athletic actions in real time. One possible way to do this would be to have athletes imagine various maneuvers, and press a button at the moments their imagined maneuvers begin and end. We could then measure to what extent the durations of imagined actions corresponded with real actions. If imagined actions are in fact much slower than real actions, then we could go on to test whether this too-slow imagination is detrimental. We might have some people imagine only complex events that cannot be imagined as quickly as real time, and have other people imagine only simple events that can be imagined as quickly as real time, and then assess any improvement (or worsening) in actually executing the skill. If it turns out that too slow imagery is detrimental, we've learned something useful!

Each paper's topic must correspond with concurrent course topic.

For each of the four relevance papers, the implications must draw from material in the corresponding, concurrent course topics. Thus, the first relevance paper must draw implications from topics studied in weeks 1-4, such as perception and attention, etc. The second paper must draw implications from topics studied during weeks 5-8, such as memory and imagery. And so on for the third and fourth papers.

Implications from non-concurrent course topics will not be given credit! If, for example, you write about implications from attentional research for your third relevance paper, you won't get credit.

The main purpose of this requirement is to make sure you consider real-life implications from material throughout the course.

Paper proposals and discussions.

Before each paper is due, you must write a very brief proposal. In the proposal, describe the life situation and two implications from cognitive psychology. You only need to make very brief descriptions in your proposal; just a sentence or two for each part of the paper.

Make two copies of your proposal, which you should bring to class the day that the proposal is due. You hand in one copy at the beginning of class. Keep the other copy for classroom discussion.

In classroom discussion, we will split up into groups. Each member of the group will briefly tell the group about his or her proposal. The other members provide constructive feedback about the proposal: Does the proposer understand the experiment or theory correctly? Does the application to a real-life situation make sense? Is the test of the implication appropriate?

After all members have described their proposals, the group will decide which proposed implication is the most viable and interesting. Your group decides exactly what is "interesting", but often an implication is interesting if it is unexpected or somehow makes a quantum leap from the laboratory to life. After all the groups have decided on their most interesting proposals, the author of each group's most interesting proposal will briefly summarize his or her idea to the whole class.

A primary purpose of the group discussion is for you to get informative feedback about your ideas. You will not get feedback from the instructor or assistant on the written proposal you hand in. Therefore, make sure that you present your ideas clearly and get genuine evaluative feedback from the members of your group. (The copy you hand in is just for administrative purposes, so you get the points for completing the proposal.)

Criteria for grading of papers and proposals.

Proposals that are (1) turned in on time (at the beginning of the appropriate class) and (2) complete in content (including a life situation and two implications) will be awarded 3 points. Proposals are not evaluated for quality or accuracy of content. No written feedback is supplied by the instructor or assistant on the proposals; such feedback occurs during the in-class group discussion.

Full papers are graded as follows. Each content section gets 0-2 points, as shown below:

  • (3 pts.) Description of cognitive aspects of a life situation.
  • Implications from cognitive psychology:
    1. Implication #1.
      • (4 pts.) Statement of theory or laboratory result.
      • (4 pts.) Specific implication for life situation.
      • (4 pts.) Possible test of the implication.
    2. Implication #2.
      • (4 pts.) Statement of theory or laboratory result.
      • (4 pts.) Specific implication for life situation.
      • (4 pts.) Possible test of the implication.

4 points are awarded to sections that clearly and concisely include all the relevant details necessary to explain the theory, implication, or test.

2 points are awarded for sections that are approximately correct, but that lack some important detail or include some erroneous statement, or that are vague or unclear.

0 points are given for sections that are essentially redundant with other sections, or which are excessively brief, vague or obscure, or which are fundamentally wrong.

The above criteria presume that the topic is concurrent with the course material (see the topic restrictions above). If the topic does not correspond with concurrent course material, no credit can be awarded.

Summary: The proposal is worth up to 3 points. The papers are worth up to 27 points. Thus, each paper assignment is worth up to 30 points total.

Hints and comments.

Common problems with papers:

  • Not drawing from concurrent course material. Your implications for each paper must draw from concurrent topics in the course. See the topic restrictions above.

  • Misunderstanding or misstating an effect or theory. Whenever you're making a connection with something discussed in class, make sure you correctly understand and clearly state that something! When describing an experimental result, be sure to describe the stimuli, task, procedure, independent variables and dependent variables. When describing a theory, be sure to describe the representation and process assumed by the theory, if possible.

  • Renaming the same point two or three times, trying to make several implications out of one. The three different implications need to be three different implications.

  • Improperly using "affect" and "effect":

    AFFECT EFFECT
    Noun "affect" as a noun means an emotion. This usage is uncommon in everday conversation, but does occur often in technical writing. When spoken, the first syllable is stressed.

    The actor conveyed intense affect.

    		 "effect" as a noun means a change, or a consequence.

    The manipulation produced a significant effect.
    Verb "affect" as a verb means to influence. When spoken, the second syllable is stressed.

    Depth of processing affects memory.

    		 "effect" as a verb means to bring about, or to bring into being. This usage is fairly rare.

    Utilization of interactive imagery effects correct recall.

  • Piling on more than two implications. You can probably think of dozens of possible implications, but choose the best two. Think deeply about these bests ones, and then write carefully about them. You only get credit for the first two implications you describe.

  • Saying way too much or too little about a theory or experiment. Say just enough to express clearly exactly what you think the application is. Please do not "parrot" the whole complex design of an experiment just to show you know it; you'd actually just be showing a lack of discretion and lack of clarity. On the other hand, don't just "telegraph" a topic, leaving important aspects merely implied. Be explicit about exactly what you think the important point is, but don't pack it with unnecessary baggage.
